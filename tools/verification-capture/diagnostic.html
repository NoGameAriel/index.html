Step 1 — Name the System
What are you examining?
Examples: "my home inspector," "FDA supplement oversight," "this news outlet's pharma coverage," "Transparent Labs"
Step 2 — The Four Questions
```
01
Who funds the verifier?
Does the money come from the entity being verified, or from a neutral source with no stake in the outcome?
02
Who controls their future work?
Even when payment is clean, referrals and reappointment are leverage. Does the subject or its network control the verifier's pipeline?
03
How quickly does reality correct the system?
Captured systems often look clean until failure. Do errors surface internally, or only through crisis?
04
Is disagreement preserved?
Healthy verification produces variance. Do reviewers dissent and flag problems — or does everything consistently align?
```

Diagnostic Result
What the results mean
Structurally Sound
The architecture is working.
Funding, access, feedback, and disagreement all point toward independence. This doesn't guarantee individual honesty — but the structure is designed correctly. You can trust the signal more, though continued observation matters.
Signal Weakened
Proceed with caution.
Some indicators of independence exist but others are compromised or opaque. The system may still produce accurate results — but you can't fully rely on it without corroboration from a structurally independent source.
Structurally Compromised
The signal is at risk.
Multiple capture indicators are present. The verifier's survival is structurally linked to the entity being verified. This doesn't mean every result is wrong — but the system cannot self-correct, and favorable outcomes should not be trusted at face value.
This is a diagnostic tool, not a verdict. It identifies structural risk, not individual wrongdoing. Use it to ask better questions — not to make final judgments.
